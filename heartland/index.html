The Heart of It

The Questions That Matter

1 Strategy & Operations (The Factorio Lens)

What recurring workflows at Heartland could be automated reliably today?
+

Weekly communications, visitor follow-up sequences, volunteer reminders, social media content, event logistics, prayer request routing, attendance trend analysis, sermon series graphics and slide decks. These are all solvable now.

If you could build a 'machine' that ran a process perfectly every time, where would you build it first?
+

Think of it like Factorio or an RTS game — you're optimizing resource flows. Every manual process is a bottleneck. Every one you automate frees a human to do something only a human can do.

How do you measure the ROI of ministry? And does AI change the equation?
+

Churches don't measure profit, but you do measure impact — lives changed, people connected, community served. If AI frees up 20 hours a week and those hours go toward actual pastoral care, that's a measurable win.

2 Stewardship & Resources

If people are giving sacrificially to the church, is it better stewardship to pay a human $50K/year for work that AI could do for $50/month?
+

This isn't hypothetical. Newsletter writing, social media scheduling, visitor follow-up emails, event promotion, volunteer scheduling — AI handles all of these well today. So is paying someone to do them the best use of the resources people are giving?

How big a staff does it really take to run a church of Heartland's size in 2026?
+

The answer was different in 2020. It was different in 2015. The tools have changed. Staff size assumptions built for a pre-AI world might not be the best use of the resources people are giving to the church.

What should volunteers do vs. what should staff do vs. what should AI do?
+

The old model was: pay staff for the skilled work, recruit volunteers for hands-on service. But if AI can do the skilled cognitive work, what should staff be focused on? And if you took admin off their plate, what could they do with that time instead?

What does 'good stewardship of human capital' mean when machines can handle cognitive work?
+

What if the best thing you could do for a staff member is take the newsletter off their plate so they can actually sit with someone who's hurting? AI can handle the newsletter. It can't sit across the table from someone.

3 What Makes Us Human?

What kind of work at Heartland requires a uniquely human touch — and who decides that boundary?
+

A sermon needs the pastor's lived experience. But what about the email that invites someone to hear it? The graphic that promotes it? The follow-up text to a first-time guest? Does all of that need a human too?

If AI can produce a devotional that moves someone to tears, does it matter that a human didn't write it?
+

We already sing songs written by people we've never met. We read translations of ancient texts. So where's the line exactly? At what point does the tool matter more than the effect?

What does it mean to use our God-given gifts if machines can do the same work faster?
+

Paul wrote about the body of Christ — each part with its function. If AI handles the administration, does that free the body to do more relational work? Or does it weaken muscles we were meant to exercise?

Can someone form a meaningful relationship with an AI spiritual companion — or is that inherently dangerous?
+

People already journal, pray with apps, and read daily devotionals written by strangers. So where's the line between a helpful tool and a counterfeit relationship?

4 Theology & Philosophy

Is there a theology of AI? Should there be?
+

We've got theologies of work, money, technology, and media. AI touches all of them. The World Council of Churches and the Vatican have already weighed in. Where does Heartland stand?

What does the Imago Dei mean in a world where machines can create, reason, and even 'care'?
+

If creativity and reasoning were what made us God's image-bearers, AI would be a real problem. But relationship, love, sacrifice, moral agency — machines can't do that part. That's still us.

Could AI actually help people grow in their faith — or is that a dangerous illusion?
+

Personalized scripture plans, prayer reminders, crisis detection that flags someone for pastoral follow-up, daily encouragements tailored to where someone actually is in their walk. All of this exists today. The question is: should we use it?

What are the dangers? Where are the guardrails?
+

Unhealthy emotional attachment to AI. Theological errors in AI-generated content. Privacy with sensitive pastoral data. Looking like you care when there's no real relationship behind it. These are real risks, and they need real policies.

5 The Invitation

What if Heartland became a model for how churches engage AI thoughtfully?
+

Clint's philosophical mind, Dave's pastoral heart, Brett's technical skills — you guys have the exact right team to actually lead this conversation. And other churches are going to be watching how you handle it.

What would it look like to have an AI policy that reflects Heartland's values of being Welcoming, Real, and Transforming?
+

Only 6% of churches have a formal AI policy. Heartland could be one of the first to build one that's rooted in actual theology instead of fear or hype.
